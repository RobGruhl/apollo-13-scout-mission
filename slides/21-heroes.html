The Unsung Heroes

The People Who Made Apollo 13's Survival Possible

More Than Three Astronauts

Jim Lovell, Jack Swigert, and Fred Haise got the glory—and they earned it. But Apollo 13's survival was the work of thousands of people who worked around the clock for 6 days straight to bring them home.

The Real Numbers:

  • 🚀 3 astronauts in space
  • 👨‍💻 ~400 flight controllers across 3 shifts in Mission Control
  • 🏭 ~10,000 engineers at NASA centers and contractors
  • 🏢 200+ companies providing support and solutions
  • 🌍 Millions watching worldwide, holding their breath
🎮 The Flight Directors - The Men Who Never Slept

Gene Kranz (Lead Flight Director - White Team)

The Legend:

  • ⏱️ On duty when explosion happened
  • 💪 Immediately took command, calmed the room
  • 📝 Famous line: "Failure is not an option"
  • 🧠 Orchestrated overall rescue strategy
  • 😴 Slept ~4 hours total over 6 days
  • 🏆 Later awarded Presidential Medal of Freedom
"When that alarm went off, I made a decision: we weren't losing this crew. I didn't care what it took. We were bringing them home." — Gene Kranz

Glynn Lunney (Gold Team Flight Director)

  • 🌙 Led the critical period after explosion
  • 💡 Developed the free-return trajectory plan
  • 🧮 Calculated power budgets for survival
  • ⏱️ Worked 18-hour shifts for 6 days straight
  • 🎯 Made real-time decisions that saved lives
"We didn't have time to think about failure. Every decision was: Will this keep them alive for one more hour? Good, do it. Next problem." — Glynn Lunney

Gerry Griffin (Gold Team Flight Director)

  • 📊 Managed mid-course corrections and trajectory planning
  • 🎯 Oversaw critical PC+2 burn behind the Moon
  • ⏱️ Coordinated between teams during shift changes
  • 🧠 Ensured continuity across flight director handovers

Milt Windler (Maroon Team Flight Director)

  • 🌍 Led re-entry planning operations
  • 🔋 Managed power-up procedures development
  • 📝 Coordinated hundreds of procedure changes
  • ⏱️ Oversaw final 24 hours before splashdown
👨‍💻 The Flight Controllers - The Problem Solvers

Sy Liebergot (EECOM - Electrical, Environmental, Consumables Manager)

  • ⚡ First to see the electrical anomaly
  • 🚨 Diagnosed the oxygen tank failure
  • 🔋 Developed power conservation strategies
  • 💧 Calculated water and oxygen budgets
  • 😰 Carried weight of having been on duty when it happened
  • 💪 Worked 72 hours straight to find solutions
"When I saw that oxygen pressure dropping, I knew. Something catastrophic had happened. And we had minutes to figure out what to do." — Sy Liebergot

John Aaron (EECOM Backroom)

  • 💡 Developed creative solutions for power management
  • 🔋 Calculated exact power budgets for every scenario
  • 📊 Monitored every amp-hour of battery consumption
  • 🧮 His calculations had zero margin for error—and they were perfect

Deke Slayton (Director of Flight Crew Operations)

  • 👨‍🚀 Former Mercury astronaut
  • 📞 Primary communicator with the crew
  • 🎯 Translated technical solutions into actionable procedures
  • 💪 Kept crew morale up during darkest moments
  • 😭 Stayed awake for nearly all 6 days, terrified he'd lose his guys

Chuck Deiterich (RETRO - Retrofire Officer)

  • 🎯 Calculated all trajectory corrections
  • 🌙 Designed the free-return trajectory modifications
  • 📐 Computed burn durations and directions
  • 🎯 Re-entry corridor calculations (±2.15° margin)
  • 🏆 His math was perfect—landed 0.6 miles from target

Ed Smylie (FIDO - Flight Dynamics Officer)

  • 📍 Tracked spacecraft position constantly
  • 🎯 Coordinated with RETRO on trajectory plans
  • 📊 Monitored velocity and altitude changes
  • 🌍 Predicted splashdown locations for recovery planning
👨‍🔧 The Engineers - The Inventors

Ken Mattingly (Replacement CM Pilot)

The Man Who Should Have Been There:

  • 🦠 Removed from flight 3 days before launch (exposed to measles)
  • 😢 Watched Jack Swigert take his seat
  • 💪 When explosion happened, became a hero on the ground
  • ⏱️ 72 hours straight in the simulator
  • 🔋 Wrote the entire Command Module restart procedure from scratch
  • 🧪 Tested every sequence, timed every step
  • 📝 400+ page procedure that saved their lives
  • 😴 Did not sleep for 3 days while writing it
"That should have been me up there. When I couldn't fly, the least I could do was make damn sure they came home. So I worked. And I didn't stop until I had a procedure that would work." — Ken Mattingly

Note: Mattingly never got measles. He was immune. He'd been removed from the flight for nothing. But his work on the ground saved three lives.

Ed Smylie and the "Mailbox" Team

  • 📦 Designed the CO2 scrubber adapter ("mailbox")
  • 🧪 Used only materials known to be aboard spacecraft
  • 🔧 Built and tested prototype on Earth in real-time
  • 📞 Radioed step-by-step instructions to crew
  • ✅ Adapter worked perfectly—CO2 dropped from 14.9 to 4.6 mmHg
"We had plastic bags, cardboard, duct tape, and hose. That's it. We had to make it work or they'd die. So we made it work." — "Mailbox" team engineer

Grumman Engineers (Lunar Module Manufacturer)

  • 🚀 Aquarius was their spacecraft
  • 📊 Provided technical specs and limitations
  • 🔋 Calculated power budgets for extended operation
  • 💡 Advised on systems never intended for lifeboat use
  • 💪 Worked around the clock supporting Mission Control

Their spacecraft was asked to do the impossible. And it did.

North American Rockwell Engineers (Command Module Manufacturer)

  • 🖥️ Provided computer restart expertise
  • 🔋 Advised on battery capacity and cold-soak effects
  • 🛡️ Analyzed heat shield integrity after explosion
  • 📊 Calculated re-entry loads and stresses
👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 The Families - The Ones Who Waited

Marilyn Lovell (Jim's Wife)

  • 💔 Watched TV for 6 days, terrified
  • 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Held their four children together
  • 📰 Reporters camped on her lawn
  • 😰 Tried to stay strong for the kids while falling apart inside
  • 📞 Phone calls from NASA every few hours with updates
  • 🙏 Praying her husband would come home
"Every ring of the phone, I thought: is this the call? Is this when they tell me he's gone? For six days, I lived in that fear." — Marilyn Lovell

Mary Haise (Fred's Wife)

  • 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Four children, youngest just 3 years old
  • 😰 Knew Fred was sick with fever—would he survive re-entry?
  • 📺 Watched every TV update, hoping for good news
  • 💪 Stayed strong for her children while terrified inside

Parents and Families of Jack Swigert

  • 👨‍👦 Jack was single, no wife or children
  • 👵 His mother and family in Colorado watched and prayed
  • 😢 His first spaceflight—and it might be his last
  • 📞 NASA kept them updated, but fear was constant

These families lived 6 days of hell, waiting to find out if their loved ones would come home. They had no control. They could only wait, and hope, and pray.

The Numbers That Tell the Story

During the 6-Day Crisis:

  • ⏱️ Mission Control shifts: 3 teams, rotating every 8 hours
  • 👨‍💻 Flight controllers on duty: ~120 per shift
  • 😴 Average sleep per controller: 3-4 hours per night
  • 📞 Phone calls made: 10,000+ to contractors and engineers
  • 📝 Procedure changes written: 500+
  • 🧮 Calculations performed: Millions (done on slide rules and computers)
  • ☕ Coffee consumed: Estimated 2,000+ pots
  • 🚬 Cigarettes smoked: Mission Control looked like a fog bank
  • 🏢 Companies involved: 200+ providing real-time support
  • 💰 Cost of rescue: Incalculable—every resource was mobilized

The Unsung Thousands

Beyond the famous names, thousands more contributed:

  • 👨‍🔬 MIT engineers: Provided computer expertise
  • 🏭 Factory workers: On call 24/7 for parts and information
  • 📡 Tracking station operators: Worldwide network maintaining communication
  • 🚢 Navy recovery teams: USS Iwo Jima and support ships
  • 🚁 Helicopter pilots: Practiced recovery for days
  • 🏊 Navy swimmers: Ready to pull crew from ocean
  • 🏥 Medical teams: Prepared for every possible injury
  • 📰 Press officers: Kept world informed
  • 👨‍🍳 Mission Control cafeteria staff: Kept controllers fed 24/7
  • 🧹 Janitors: Kept facilities running while everyone else worked

Every single person played a role. From the engineers calculating trajectories to the cafeteria workers making sandwiches at 3 AM, everyone was part of bringing Apollo 13 home.

"People ask me how we saved Apollo 13. I tell them: we didn't. WE did. Thousands of us. Everyone who touched that mission, everyone who cared, everyone who refused to give up. That's how Apollo 13 survived. Because failure wasn't an option. Not for any of us." — Gene Kranz
