NASA's Decision

‚úì ATTEMPT THE JUMP-START

Mission Control had no choice: "We're going to attempt the LM-to-CM power transfer. It's never been done, but it's the only option."

The Emergency Procedure:

Houston designed a brand-new power-up procedure:

  • Step 1: Connect LM batteries to CM charging system
  • Step 2: Carefully monitor voltage and current
  • Step 3: Transfer power slowly to avoid surges
  • Step 4: Watch for short circuits or battery failures
  • Step 5: Charge CM batteries to sufficient levels

Engineers held their breath. This had never been tested. Not in simulators. Not on the ground. Never.

The Execution:

Jack Swigert executed the procedure step by step, calling out voltages and currents. Mission Control tracked every amp transferred.

The Close Call:

During the transfer, one CM battery briefly "dropped off the line"‚Äîthe voltage dipped and engineers feared it had failed. But it recovered. The transfer continued.

"We watched those numbers like hawks. Every volt, every amp. If we saw a short circuit, we'd have to abort. But the procedure worked. The batteries charged." ‚Äî John Aaron, EECOM (who designed the power plan)

Result: Success!

The jump-start worked perfectly.

CM batteries charged to sufficient levels for re-entry.

This procedure was added to NASA's emergency playbook.

The Hero: John Aaron

John Aaron, the EECOM (Electrical, Environmental, and Communications) flight controller, led the power plan that made this possible. He was the same engineer who saved Apollo 12 with "SCE to AUX" after a lightning strike. Now, he saved Apollo 13 with a power transfer procedure written from scratch.

Why This Mattered:

  • ‚úì Re-entry made possible: Without this, crew would have died
  • ‚úì Computer power: Guidance system had sufficient power
  • ‚úì Parachute deployment: Enough power for pyrotechnics
  • ‚úì Survival margin: Extra power for unexpected issues

Why it was the right decision: There was no alternative. The jump-start was risky, untested, and could have failed catastrophically. But 2% power was certain death. They gambled on ingenuity and it paid off. This made re-entry possible.
