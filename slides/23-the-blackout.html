Chapter 6: The Silence

The Re-Entry Blackout

GET 142:40:46 - 142:44:36 | 4 Minutes of Silence

What is Re-Entry Blackout?

When a spacecraft enters Earth's atmosphere at 25,000 mph, friction with air molecules creates a superheated plasma sheath around the capsule. This plasma is ionized gas—electrons stripped from atoms—and it blocks all radio signals.

Why Radio Stops Working:

  • 🔥 Plasma temperature: 5,000°F+ surrounding spacecraft
  • 📡 Radio waves can't penetrate: Ionized plasma reflects radio signals
  • 🚫 Complete communication loss: No voice, no telemetry, no data
  • ⏱️ Duration: Typically 3-4 minutes
  • ❓ Ground's perspective: Total silence—no idea if crew is alive

Normal Blackout Duration:

For typical Apollo missions returning from the Moon:

  • Expected blackout: 3 minutes, 30 seconds
  • Maximum ever seen: 3 minutes, 45 seconds
  • If longer than 4 minutes: Something has gone wrong

For Apollo 13, blackout was expected to last ~3 minutes, 30 seconds.

GET 142:40:46 - Entry Interface

What the Crew Experienced:

Altitude: 400,000 feet (76 miles)

Odyssey hit the top of the atmosphere. Not a hard impact—atmosphere doesn't have a clear boundary. But the spacecraft began to feel it:

  • 🌬️ First sensation: Slight buffeting, like driving on a bumpy road
  • 📈 G-forces building: Starting at 0.05 G, ramping up quickly
  • 🔊 Sound: Low rumble growing louder
  • 😰 Crew status: Strapped in, silent, waiting

Jack Swigert's last words before blackout:

"We're getting some Gs, Houston." — GET 142:40:50

Then... static. Radio dead. Silence.

Inside Odyssey - The Descent

Altitude 350,000 feet - 30 Seconds Into Re-Entry:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 1,500°F and climbing
  • 📈 G-forces: 2 Gs—crew feels twice their normal weight
  • 🔥 Window view: Orange glow starting to appear outside
  • 💪 Breathing: Harder with chest compressed by Gs
  • 🎮 Computer: Firing RCS thrusters to maintain re-entry angle

Altitude 300,000 feet - 1 Minute:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 3,000°F
  • 📈 G-forces: 4 Gs—arms feel like they weigh 40 pounds each
  • 🔥 Window view: Bright orange plasma, like looking into a furnace
  • 💨 Ablative shield working: Heat shield charring, burning away by design
  • 😓 Crew: Grunting from G-load, can barely move arms

Altitude 250,000 feet - 1.5 Minutes:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 4,000°F
  • 📈 G-forces: 6 Gs—peak deceleration
  • 🔥 Window view: Blinding white-yellow plasma
  • 💨 Ablative material: Vaporizing, carrying heat away (working as designed)
  • 🔊 Sound: Roaring like a freight train
  • 😣 Crew: Can barely breathe, vision graying from G-forces

Altitude 200,000 feet - 2 Minutes:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 5,000°F (PEAK)
  • 📈 G-forces: 6 Gs sustained
  • 🔥 Heat shield status: Still intact (so far...)
  • 😰 Crew concern: "Is the heat shield holding? Any cracks? Will we know before we die?"

The Terrifying Thought:

If the heat shield had been cracked by the explosion, they wouldn't know until plasma started pouring through the hull. And then they'd have about 2 seconds to realize they were going to die.

They rode it down in silence, waiting to find out if they'd live or die.

Altitude 150,000 feet - 2.5 Minutes:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 4,500°F (cooling down)
  • 📈 G-forces: 5 Gs
  • 🔥 Plasma: Beginning to dissipate—less ionization
  • 😤 Breathing: Slightly easier as Gs reduce

Altitude 100,000 feet - 3 Minutes:

  • 🌡️ Heat shield temperature: 3,000°F
  • 📈 G-forces: 3 Gs
  • 🔥 Plasma sheath: Thinning—radio should work soon
  • 📡 Radio: Still static
  • ⏱️ Time in blackout: 3 minutes—approaching normal duration

In Mission Control - The Wait

GET 142:40:46 - Blackout Begins:

Jack Swigert's voice cut off mid-transmission. Then... nothing.

Mission Control went silent.

200+ engineers, all watching their screens. All waiting. Nobody spoke.

  • ⏱️ 1 minute: Expected. Normal.
  • ⏱️ 2 minutes: Still normal. But tension building.
  • ⏱️ 3 minutes: Should hear them any second now.
  • ⏱️ 3 minutes, 30 seconds: Expected end of blackout. Still nothing.
  • ⏱️ 3 minutes, 45 seconds: Longer than any previous Apollo blackout. Still silence.
  • ⏱️ 4 minutes: Hearts sinking. Something is wrong.

What Could Cause Extended Blackout?

  • ❌ Heat shield failure: Crew dead, capsule burning up
  • ❌ Wrong entry angle: Skipped off atmosphere into space, lost forever
  • ❌ Computer failure: Tumbling uncontrolled, wrong attitude
  • ❌ Electrical failure: Radio dead even after plasma clears
  • ⚠️ Or... just unusual plasma conditions: Maybe they're still okay?

Why Was Blackout Longer Than Expected?

Post-flight analysis showed:

  • ✓ Entry angle was slightly shallower than typical (by design for Apollo 13's configuration)
  • ✓ Shallower angle = longer time in upper atmosphere = longer plasma exposure
  • ✓ Everything worked perfectly—blackout was just 20 seconds longer than usual
  • ✓ Heat shield intact, no damage
  • ✓ Computer worked flawlessly
  • ✓ Trajectory perfect

Those extra 20 seconds felt like 20 years to everyone waiting on Earth.
