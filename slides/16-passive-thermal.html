The Solution: Passive Thermal Control (PTC)

Apollo spacecraft used a simple but brilliant solution: rotate slowly so all sides get equal exposure to Sun and shadow.

ğŸ”„ How the "Barbecue Roll" Works

The Maneuver:

  • Rotation axis: Aligned with spacecraft's long axis (like a rotisserie chicken)
  • Rotation rate: ~3 revolutions per hour (one complete rotation every 20 minutes)
  • Method: Fire small RCS (Reaction Control System) thrusters to start spin
  • Maintenance: Occasional thruster pulses to maintain rotation rate
  • Duration: Continuous from Earth departure until re-entry prep

Why It Works:

  • Average temperature stays moderate and safe

Problem #1: Debris Cloud

The explosion created thousands of ice particles and debris pieces orbiting with the spacecraft. Normal PTC uses star sightings to maintain orientation, but stars were invisible through the debris cloud.

Problem #2: Venting Thrust

Oxygen and water continuously venting from the damaged Service Module acted like tiny rocket engines, pushing the spacecraft off-course and disrupting the rotation.

  • ğŸ’¨ Uneven venting caused wobble in rotation

Problem #3: Limited RCS Fuel

Every thruster firing to maintain PTC burned precious fuel needed for course corrections and re-entry alignment. The crew had to balance:

  • Maintaining rotation for thermal control
  • Conserving fuel for critical maneuvers later

Problem #4: Coupled Spacecraft Configuration

Apollo 13 was flying in an unusual configuration:

  • LM "Aquarius" (active, crew inside)
  • CM "Odyssey" (powered down, attached to LM)
  • SM (damaged, venting, still attached)

This created unbalanced mass distribution, making PTC harder to maintain smoothly.

Solution:

Mission Control calculated modified PTC procedures using Sun angle instead of star sightings. The crew had to manually monitor rotation with stopwatch and adjust with thruster pulses every 2-3 hours.

The Manual Process

Every 2-3 Hours, the Crew Had To:

  1. Check rotation rate: Time how long one full rotation takes
  2. Compare to target: Should be 20 minutes per rotation
  3. Calculate correction: Determine thruster pulse duration needed
  4. Fire thrusters: Short pulse to adjust rotation
  5. Monitor result: Watch for next few rotations
  6. Record fuel usage: Track RCS propellant consumption
  7. Report to Houston: Update Mission Control on status

This became routine background work that had to be done every few hours for 4 days straight, in addition to all other survival tasks.
