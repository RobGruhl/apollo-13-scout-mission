John Aaron: Steely-Eyed Missile Man

The Engineer Who Saved Two Missions

Who Was John Aaron?

  • Full Name: John W. Aaron
  • Born: 1943, rural western Oklahoma
  • Education: Physics degree (1964) from Southwestern State College, Weatherford, OK
  • NASA Career: Joined NASA in 1964, became legendary flight controller
  • Role: EECOM (Electrical, Environmental, and Communications systems)
  • EECOM duties: Power, oxygen, cooling, sensors, telemetry to Mission Control

Apollo 12: "SCE to AUX"

⚡ The Lightning Strike That Should Have Ended the Mission

November 14, 1969 - 36 Seconds After Liftoff:

Two lightning strikes hit Apollo 12 during ascent. The spacecraft's data went completely haywire. Mission Control's screens filled with gibberish. Controllers were blind to power and fuel-cell data. An abort seemed inevitable.

The Problem:

  • ⚡ Lightning scrambled telemetry data
  • ⚡ Screens showed random, meaningless numbers
  • ⚡ Flight Director couldn't see power or fuel status
  • ⚡ Standard procedure: ABORT

John Aaron's Split-Second Call:

Watching his EECOM screens, Aaron recognized a specific telemetry pattern. He'd seen it before—once—during a simulation when the Signal Conditioning Equipment (SCE) lost its normal power supply.

Aaron to Flight Director:

"Flight, try SCE to AUX."

CAPCOM relayed it to the crew. Nobody on board knew what SCE was.

Alan Bean (Lunar Module Pilot): "What's that?"

Then Bean remembered—a tiny switch on Panel 8. He flipped it to AUX.

Telemetry came back. Data was clean. Mission saved.

Why This Mattered:

Without that switch flip, controllers were blind. They would have aborted the mission. Instead, Apollo 12 went on to complete a perfect lunar landing. All because John Aaron:

  • ✓ Remembered an obscure simulation from months earlier
  • ✓ Recognized a pattern no one else saw
  • ✓ Acted with confidence under extreme pressure
  • ✓ Made a call that saved the mission in seconds

The Nickname:

NASA Flight Director Gerry Griffin called Aaron a "steely-eyed missile man"—the highest compliment in Mission Control. The nickname stuck.

Apollo 13: The Power Plan

🔋 Leading the Power & Consumables Strategy

After the Explosion:

When Apollo 13's oxygen tank exploded, Flight Director Gene Kranz made a critical decision: Put John Aaron in charge of ALL power and consumables for both spacecraft.

Aaron's Authority:

  • 💡 Complete control: Every watt on both spacecraft
  • 💡 Veto power: Could reject any checklist item that used too much power
  • 💡 Master strategist: Shaped every decision around power budget
  • 💡 Team leadership: Coordinated with Arnie Aldrich (master checklists) and Bill Peters (LM survival systems)

The Challenges:

The crew needed power for 90+ hours to reach Earth, then enough to power up the Command Module for re-entry. Aaron had to:

  • 📊 Calculate exact power budget for entire mission
  • 📊 Account for every amp-hour remaining
  • 📊 Build minimal power-up sequence for frozen CM
  • 📊 Design LM-to-CM battery charging procedure (never done before)
  • 📊 Leave margin for unexpected emergencies

The Tools: Slide Rules and Graph Paper

This was 1970. No computers for controllers. Aaron's team calculated power budgets by hand:

  • 📐 Slide rules for multiplication and division
  • 📐 Graph paper to track power over time
  • 📐 Reference manuals for every system's power draw
  • 📐 Coffee, determination, and zero margin for error

The Battery Charging Plan:

One of Aaron's team's greatest innovations: charging the Command Module batteries from the Lunar Module. This had never been attempted.

The Problem: CM batteries drained to ~2% during 4-day power-down. Not enough for re-entry.

The Solution: Aaron's team created a procedure to transfer LM battery power to CM batteries. Engineers Jim Kelly and Bill Peters worked out the details. Ken Mattingly tested it in simulators.

It worked. Without this, the crew would have died during re-entry.

The Re-Entry Power Budget:

Available power: ~43 amp-hours (after 4 days)

Required for re-entry: ~40 amp-hours minimum

Margin for error: ~3 amp-hours (7%)

Aaron's power plan left just enough margin. At splashdown, batteries had ~20% remaining. Any less, and the crew would have died.

Result: They Made It Home

John Aaron's power budget was perfect. Every calculation correct. Every decision sound. The crew had just enough electricity to light the guidance computer and re-enter safely.

Career After Apollo

🚀 From Flight Controller to NASA Leadership
  • Skylab (1973-1974): Headed EECOM section, training controllers for America's first space station
  • Space Shuttle (1970s-1980s): Moved to Orbiter development; became project manager for Shuttle flight software (5-computer software-voting flight control system)
  • Space Station Freedom (1984-1993): Served as deputy and later manager at JSC for Space Station projects
  • Later roles: Systems engineering and program leadership at JSC, assignments with NASA HQ and JPL
  • Retirement: Left NASA in 2000 after 36 years of service

Recognition & Legacy

Presidential Medal of Freedom:

John Aaron received the Presidential Medal of Freedom as part of the Apollo 13 Mission Operations Team. President Nixon awarded the medal to the team for their extraordinary efforts in bringing the crew home safely.

In Popular Culture:

  • 🎬 Portrayed by Loren Dean in the film Apollo 13 (1995)
  • 📺 Featured in documentaries like Mission Control: The Unsung Heroes of Apollo
  • 📚 Mentioned in countless books about the Apollo program

Why John Aaron Matters

Three Principles of Excellence:

1. Pattern Recognition Under Stress

Aaron didn't panic when Apollo 12's data went crazy. He recognized a pattern he'd seen once before. That split-second recognition saved the mission.

2. Systems Thinking

On Apollo 13, Aaron treated power as the mission's master constraint. Every decision flowed from that understanding. He built checklists around it, vetoed actions that violated it, and made sure every watt counted.

3. Team Orchestration

Aaron didn't work alone. He integrated efforts from flight directors, subsystem leads, simulator teams, and engineers across NASA. Ken Mattingly verified procedures in simulators. Jim Kelly and Bill Peters worked battery charging. Aaron coordinated it all.

"John Aaron represents the best of NASA: brilliant technical knowledge, calm under pressure, decisive action, and the humility to work as part of a team. He saved two missions. He saved six lives. He's the engineer everyone hopes to be." — Gene Kranz, Apollo Flight Director
